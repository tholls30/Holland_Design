SoulCycle

Full-time position focused on increasing new rider attrition rates.

Project overview

"We ride together as a pack in candlelit studios to the rhythm of one-of-a-kind playlists."

The Problem

When I was hired at SoulCycle I was placed on a team dedicated to increasing the conversion rate of visitors into new customers or "riders". My first order of business upon arrival was to learn how the organization operated as whole and how they advertised or sold the business to potential new riders. After a couple months of learning, investigating, and user research the team and I determined that there were two major barriers to entry. The first issue was with the website; upon reaching the homepage there was no mention of what SoulCycle was or what a class was like. There were also no clear calls-to-action (CTAs) that pulled potential new riders into the sign-up process or even brought them to pertinent information about the classes that were offered. Secondly, if you as a potential new rider wanted to sign up there was no step-by-step process to help you choose the best class for you. This was especially a large problem in the NYC market that had over 1000 classes to chose from for any given week.

The Process

As we began upon our journey of attempting to remedy these problems myself, the product manager, and the engineering lead had daily conversations about what was happening and what our next steps were. This mainly served to keep everyone on the team informed about what was in the works and to push the project forward. After determining the key user pain points or barriers to entry we conferred with the VP of product and marketing to make sure that everyone was in agreeance with the core problems. As we moved forward we had bi-weekly meetings with a cross-functional team from all aspects of the business as we wanted to completely re-do the homepage which, as with any business, was a high profile page. We presented our findings and progress each week as we iterated on the designs in close collaboration with the marketing & content teams. Once we had a design and information that was agreed upon by the cross-functional team we performed a series of user interview and A/B tests on the old homepage and the new one in order to determine if we had actually made it more persuasive and easier to sign up for the first time. Around this point we also received a mandate that the new homepage should be built upon a CMS system which made sense, but it also extended the process as we had to vet multiple vendors and iterate on the design to fit the engineering capabilities of the new CMS system.

The Conclusion

Up to this point I had primarily worked at start-up companies that were very open and communicative about creating features and designs that would further the business. Unfortunately this was not the case at SoulCycle as it was primarily lead through politics and egos. The homepage went from something that most agreed (and research confirmed) would dramatically increase our attrition rate of new riders to basically a video reel and quick message and then the footer. We managed to implement the CMS and two CTAs successfully, but at this point I already knew this was not an organization that I wanted to stay at for the long term. Upon delivering the approved design I decided to move out to Colorado and start freelancing.

Additional Work

During my time at SoulCycle I also helped improve the onboarding process of the iOS app by walking new users through a multi-step wizard that helped filter down the list of possible classes available to them based on their preferences. Additionally I also worked very closely with a few engineers and the rest of the design team to create a design system that took into account all the mediums we worked on including all retail and marketing initiatives.
